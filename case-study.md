# Case Study: UPI / Online Payment Fraud

**CSE0001 – Digital Literacy | Task 5 – Part A**

---

## Type of Cybercrime: UPI / Online Payment Fraud

---

## What Is It?

UPI (Unified Payments Interface) fraud is a form of financial cybercrime where an attacker tricks a victim into authorising a payment or revealing sensitive information — such as a UPI PIN, OTP, or linked bank details — through deception. Unlike traditional bank fraud, UPI fraud is fast, often irreversible, and increasingly targets young people and first-time digital payment users.

---

## Realistic Case Scenario

Arjun had been saving up since Class 11. The ₹18,000 he was hoping to get for his old laptop wasn't just money — it was meant to cover his first semester's worth of printing costs, lab supplies, and maybe one decent meal out with his new college friends. He posted in his college's Facebook group on a Tuesday evening, feeling good about the plan.

By 9 PM, a message arrived on WhatsApp. The profile photo showed a man in uniform. He introduced himself as an Army officer posted in Pune, said he needed a laptop urgently for his son, and offered to pay the full amount immediately via Google Pay — no bargaining, no delay. Arjun felt relieved. This was easier than he expected.

The man sent a QR code and explained patiently: "Just scan this and enter your UPI PIN to confirm your account so I can send the payment." It sounded reasonable. Arjun had only started using UPI a few months ago. He scanned the code, entered his PIN, and waited for the notification that money had arrived.

The notification came — but it said ₹14,500 debited.

He typed a panicked message to the buyer. It showed a single grey tick. Within seconds, he was blocked. Arjun sat staring at his phone screen for a long moment before calling his mother, not sure how to explain what had just happened. By the time he reached his bank's helpline, the money was gone.

---

## How It Happened — Step by Step

1. Attacker identified Arjun as a target through a public post on social media.
2. Built trust by posing as an Army officer (a common impersonation tactic in India).
3. Sent a **collect/debit QR code** — which, when scanned and confirmed with a PIN, deducts money from the victim's account, not deposits it.
4. Convinced Arjun that entering his UPI PIN was a necessary step to "receive" payment.
5. Once the transaction was approved, the money was gone and the attacker disappeared.

---

## Who Is Usually Targeted?

- First-time UPI users (students, elderly people, rural users)
- People selling items on OLX, Facebook Marketplace, or college groups
- Job seekers being asked to pay a "registration fee" via UPI
- Anyone unfamiliar with the difference between a collect request and a send request

---

## Consequences

- Immediate and often irreversible financial loss
- Psychological distress, embarrassment, and loss of trust in digital payments
- Legal complications if the victim unknowingly participates in a money mule chain
- In some cases, the fraudster uses the victim's details for further identity theft

---

## Key Lesson

**You NEVER need to enter your UPI PIN to receive money.**

If anyone asks you to scan a QR code or approve a request to get paid — it is fraud.
